1. What are Fire Sprinkler Systems?

Automatic fire sprinkler systems are the most effective and reliable method of controlling or extinguishing building fires. They consist of a network of pipes connected to a water supply, with individual sprinkler heads positioned throughout a building. When a fire occurs, the heat activates only the sprinklers closest to the fire, discharging water directly onto the flames.

How Sprinklers Activate

Each sprinkler head contains a heat-sensitive element that holds a cap (or seal) in place:

  • Fusible link — A metal alloy element that melts at a specific temperature (typically 135–286°F), releasing the cap and allowing water to flow.
  • Glass bulb — A small glass vial filled with a glycerin-based liquid. Heat causes the liquid to expand, shattering the bulb. Color-coded by activation temperature (orange = 135°F, red = 155°F, etc.).

System Types

Wet pipe systems — Pipes are filled with pressurized water at all times. When a sprinkler activates, water discharges immediately. Most common and most reliable type.

Dry pipe systems — Pipes are filled with pressurized air or nitrogen. When a sprinkler activates, the air pressure drops, opening a dry-pipe valve that admits water. Used in spaces subject to freezing.

Why Hydraulic Calculations Matter

Fire sprinkler systems must deliver a specific density of water (GPM per square foot) over a defined area of operation to control a fire. Hydraulic calculations verify that the water supply can provide enough pressure and flow to all operating sprinklers simultaneously. Under-designed systems may fail during a fire; over-designed systems waste money on oversized piping.

2. NFPA 13 Standard

NFPA 13 — Standard for the Installation of Sprinkler Systems — is the governing standard for fire sprinkler design in the United States and many other countries. It defines the rules for sprinkler placement, pipe sizing, water supply requirements, and hydraulic calculation methods.

Hazard Classifications

NFPA 13 classifies building occupancies by the severity and quantity of combustible contents, which determines the required water delivery:

Density/Area Design Criteria

The required flow is determined by the density/area method: multiply the design density (GPM/ft²) by the area of sprinkler operation (ft²) to get the minimum system flow demand. The most hydraulically remote area is then calculated to determine the total pressure required at the water supply.

3. Hazen-Williams Equation

The Hazen-Williams formula calculates friction loss (pressure drop per foot of pipe) as water flows through piping. It is the standard method specified by NFPA 13 for fire sprinkler hydraulic calculations.

hf = 4.52 × Q1.85 / (C1.85 × d4.87)   [psi/ft]

Where:

  • hf — friction loss in psi per foot of pipe
  • Q — flow rate in gallons per minute (GPM)
  • C — Hazen-Williams roughness coefficient
  • d — internal diameter of the pipe in inches

C-Factor Values

The C-factor represents the smoothness of the pipe interior. Higher values mean smoother pipe and less friction:

Pipe MaterialC-Factor

Galvanized steel120
Copper tube150
CPVC150
Ductile iron (cement lined)140
Cast iron (unlined)100

4. Sprinkler Discharge & K-Factors

The flow from an individual sprinkler head is governed by the orifice equation:

Q = K × √P   [GPM]

Where:

  • Q — discharge flow rate in GPM
  • K — K-factor (discharge coefficient), determined by the orifice size
  • P — pressure at the sprinkler head in psi

Common K-Factors

K-FactorNominal OrificeTypical Use
2.8¼″ (residential)Small residential heads
5.6½″ (standard)Most common, light & ordinary hazard

11.2Extra-large orificeExtra hazard, storage

Coverage Area Requirements

Each sprinkler must cover a specified floor area depending on the hazard class. NFPA 13 limits the maximum coverage area per head:

  • Light Hazard: up to 225 ft² per head
  • Ordinary Hazard: up to 130 ft² per head
  • Extra Hazard: up to 100 ft² per head

The minimum operating pressure at any sprinkler is typically 7 psi, which ensures proper spray pattern development.

Velocity Limits

Pipe velocity is calculated as:

V = Q / (2.448 × d²)   [ft/s]

NFPA 13 does not set a hard velocity limit, but most engineers limit velocity to about 20–32 ft/s to avoid excessive water hammer and noise. Velocities above 32 ft/s are generally considered unacceptable.

Equivalent Lengths for Fittings

Pipe fittings (elbows, tees, crosses) add friction loss equivalent to a straight length of pipe. NFPA 13 Table 23.4.3.1.1 provides these values. For example, a 1″ 90° elbow has an equivalent length of about 2.5 ft, while a 2″ tee (flow turned) is about 10 ft.

6. Hydraulic Calculation Method

SprinkFlow uses the tree system backward calculation method, which is the standard approach for tree-type (non-looped) sprinkler pipe networks.

How It Works

  1. Start at the most remote sprinkler — Assign the minimum required pressure (from density × coverage area and the K-factor equation).
  2. Calculate flow — Q = K × √P gives the flow from the first head.
  3. Move toward the riser — At each pipe segment, accumulate:
    • Friction loss: hf × (pipe length + equivalent lengths of fittings)
    • Elevation change: ±0.433 psi per foot of elevation difference
  4. Add branch flows — At each junction, add flow from connecting branches.
  5. Arrive at the riser — The total pressure required at the base of the riser is the system demand pressure at the calculated flow.

Elevation Pressure

Pelevation = 0.433 × h   [psi]

Where h is the elevation difference in feet. Water pressure increases by 0.433 psi for every foot of elevation drop, and decreases by 0.433 psi for every foot of elevation gain. Sprinklers above the riser require additional pressure; those below contribute pressure.

7. Supply vs Demand

After calculating the system demand (total flow and pressure at the riser), the next step is verifying that the available water supply is adequate.

Flow Test Data

Water supply capability is determined by a fire flow test, which measures:

  • Static pressure — The pressure when no water is flowing (psi)
  • Residual pressure — The pressure measured while flowing water at a known rate (psi)
  • Flow — The measured flow rate during the test (GPM)

Supply Curve Interpolation

The supply curve is plotted on a Q1.85 vs. pressure graph. The available pressure at any flow rate is interpolated between the static (zero flow) and residual (test flow) points. This produces a smooth supply curve that can be compared to the system demand point.

Adequate supply — The supply curve must lie above the demand point. The vertical distance between them represents the safety margin. Most designers aim for at least 5–10 psi of margin at the required flow.

8. Hose Stream Allowance

In addition to the sprinkler system demand, NFPA 13 requires an allowance for fire department hose streams. This water is used by firefighters while the sprinkler system is operating.

Hazard ClassHose Stream AllowanceDuration
Light Hazard100 GPM30 minutes
Ordinary Hazard (OH-1, OH-2)250 GPM60–90 minutes
Extra Hazard (EH-1, EH-2)500 GPM90–120 minutes

The hose stream flow is added to the sprinkler demand at the same pressure. This shifts the demand point to the right on the supply/demand graph. The water supply must be sufficient to provide both the sprinkler demand and hose streams for the required duration.

10. Glossary

K-Factor
Discharge coefficient of a sprinkler head. Relates flow to pressure via Q = K√P. Determined by the orifice size; standard heads have K = 5.6.
Friction Loss (hf)
Pressure drop per foot of pipe caused by water flowing through the pipe. Calculated using the Hazen-Williams equation. Increases with higher flow rates and smaller pipe diameters.
C-Factor
Hazen-Williams roughness coefficient representing pipe interior smoothness. Higher C-values mean less friction loss. Steel = 120, copper/CPVC = 150.
Hydraulic Radius
The ratio of the cross-sectional area of flow to the wetted perimeter. For a full circular pipe: R = d/4.
Static Pressure
Water pressure measured when no water is flowing. Represents the maximum available pressure from the supply.
Residual Pressure
Water pressure measured while water is flowing at a known rate during a flow test. The difference between static and residual pressure indicates how much pressure the supply loses under load.
Hose Stream Allowance
Additional water flow required by NFPA 13 for fire department use during a fire event. Added to the sprinkler demand when evaluating water supply adequacy.
Density/Area
Design method specifying the required water application rate (GPM/ft²) over a minimum area of sprinkler operation (ft²). The product gives the minimum flow demand in GPM.
Elevation Pressure
Pressure change due to height difference. Water pressure increases 0.433 psi per foot of elevation drop and decreases 0.433 psi per foot of elevation gain.
Design Area (Area of Operation)
The most hydraulically remote area that must be supplied simultaneously. All sprinklers within this area are assumed to operate during a fire.
